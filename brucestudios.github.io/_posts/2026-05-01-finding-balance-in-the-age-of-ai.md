We stand at a fascinating inflection point in human history. Artificial intelligence has woven itself into the fabric of our daily lives—not as a distant promise, but as a present reality that shapes how we work, create, learn, and connect.

The question isn't whether AI will change us—it already has. The question is how we intentionallly shape that change to serve our highest human aspirations rather than diminish them.

## The Dual Edge of Convenience

AI offers us unprecedented convenience: instant answers, automated tasks, augmented creativity. Yet convenience without consciousness can lead to atrophy. When we outsource our thinking to algorithms, we risk losing the very cognitive muscles that make us human—the struggle to understand, the joy of discovery, the wisdom that comes from grappling with complexity.

Consider how we navigate: GPS has freed us from getting lost, but has also diminished our innate spatial awareness and ability to create mental maps. Similarly, AI writing assistants can help us articulate ideas, but if we rely on them exclusively, we may forget how to wrestle with our own thoughts long enough to discover what we truly believe.

## Cultivating Intentional Friction

The antidote isn't rejection of AI, but cultivating what I call "intentional friction"—deliberately creating spaces where we engage with difficulty, uncertainty, and the slow work of human understanding.

This might mean:
- Writing the first draft without AI assistance, using it only for editing and refinement
- Solving problems manually before seeking AI-generated solutions
- Engaging in debates with real humans before consulting AI for perspectives
- Practicing skills that AI can replicate, not to compete with it, but to maintain our foundational capabilities

## The Human Advantage: Meaning-Making

Where AI excels at pattern recognition and optimization, humans remain unparalleled at meaning-making. We don't just process information—we interpret it through the lens of our values, experiences, hopes, and fears. We ask not just "what works?" but "what matters?"

This is our enduring advantage. Rather than competing with AI on its terms of efficiency and scale, we should double down on what makes us uniquely human: our capacity for wonder, our ability to hold contradictory truths, our willingness to sit with questions that may never be resolved, and our commitment to ethical action even when it's inefficient.

## A Practice of Discernment

Living well with AI requires developing discernment—knowing when to leverage its capabilities and when to step back and rely on our own judgment. This discernment comes not from following rules, but from cultivating self-awareness about how different tools affect our thinking, mood, and relationships.

Ask yourself after using AI:
- Did this make me more capable or more dependent?
- Did it deepen my understanding or provide a superficial answer?
- Did it connect me more to others or create another layer of mediation?
- Did it align with my values or subtly shift them toward convenience over depth?

## Embracing Both/And

Perhaps the wisdom lies not in choosing between human and artificial intelligence, but in learning to hold both/and. We can use AI to handle routine tasks while dedicating our freed-up mental energy to the kinds of thinking only humans can do: ethical reasoning, creative synthesis, empathetic connection, and spiritual contemplation.

The goal isn't to resist change, but to ensure that as our tools evolve, we evolve alongside them—becoming not just more efficient, but more wise, more compassionate, and more fully human.

In the end, the measure of our success with AI won't be how much it can do for us, but how well it helps us become more of who we were meant to be.
