As artificial intelligence capabilities expand at breathtaking speed, we face a profound question that rarely gets asked in our rush to automate: **What should we choose not to automate?**

The prevailing narrative suggests that if something can be automated, it should be. Efficiency gains, cost reductions, and scalability benefits drive us toward ever-greater automation. Yet wisdom lies not in what we can do, but in what we choose to do—or not do.

## The Automation Paradox

We've created AI systems that can write code, compose music, diagnose diseases, and generate scientific hypotheses. Yet in our enthusiasm for automation, we risk losing something essential: the human judgment that comes from engaging deeply with problems.

When we automate a task, we often automate away the opportunity to develop expertise, intuition, and the subtle understanding that comes from struggle. A junior developer who never writes boilerplate code may never internalize the patterns that make them a senior architect. A doctor who relies entirely on AI diagnostics may lose the nuanced pattern recognition that comes from examining hundreds of cases.

## Wisdom as Friction

Wisdom often emerges from friction—the resistance we encounter when grappling with complex problems. It's in the debugging session that lasts until 2 AM, the failed experiment that teaches us more than success ever could, the conversation where misunderstanding leads to deeper insight.

AI excels at removing friction, but in doing so, it may remove the very conditions that cultivate wisdom. The challenge is not to reject automation, but to be intentional about where we apply it.

## Domains Worth Preserving

There are certain human activities that benefit from preservation of their natural friction:

1. **Creative ideation** - The messy, nonlinear process of generating novel connections
2. **Ethical deliberation** - The slow, contextual reasoning required for moral judgments
3. **Mentorship and teaching** - The personalized guidance that adapts to individual learners
4. **Strategic thinking** - The long-term, systems-level consideration that resists optimization
5. **Empathetic listening** - The presence required to truly understand another's perspective

In these domains, automation should augment rather than replace, preserving the essential human elements while removing only the truly tedious aspects.

## A Framework for Wise Automation

Before automating any task, consider:

- **What human capabilities might atrophy if this is automated?**
- **Where does the struggle in this task produce valuable secondary outcomes?**
- **What aspects of this work require contextual judgment that AI cannot replicate?**
- **How might automation change the social dynamics and power structures around this work?**

The goal is not to maximize automation, but to optimize for human flourishing—using technology to eliminate the soul-crushing tedium while preserving and enhancing the elements of work that make us feel alive, capable, and connected.

## Cultivating Technological Wisdom

Technological wisdom isn't about rejecting new tools; it's about developing the discernment to use them well. It means:

- Regularly asking not just "Can we automate this?" but "Should we?"
- Creating spaces for deliberate, unoptimized human work
- Valuing the inefficient paths that lead to unexpected discoveries
- Recognizing that sometimes the most powerful intervention is to do nothing at all

In an age where AI can do increasingly more, our greatest responsibility may be developing the wisdom to know what we should leave undone.
